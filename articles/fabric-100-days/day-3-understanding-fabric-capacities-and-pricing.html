Microsoft Fabric Pricing and Capacity Units (CUs) — Complete Guide

Understanding how Fabric is priced is essential before you commit to a SKU. Unlike Azure's traditional per-service billing (separate meters for Spark, SQL, pipelines, Power BI), Fabric uses a single currency: Capacity Units (CUs). Everything your workloads consume is measured in CUs.

What is a Capacity Unit (CU)?

A Capacity Unit is Fabric's compute currency. When you run a Spark notebook, execute a SQL query, refresh a Power BI semantic model, or run a pipeline, those operations consume CUs from your capacity's pool.

Key properties:

  • CUs are shared across all Fabric workloads in workspaces assigned to a capacity
  • Each SKU tier provides a fixed number of CUs per hour
  • Fabric uses smoothing — short bursts can temporarily exceed the CU limit, with the overage paid back over the next 24 hours
  • When a capacity is paused, you pay nothing (pay-as-you-go) or the reservation continues (reserved pricing)

The CU Smoothing Algorithm — How Burst Works

Fabric does not immediately throttle you when you exceed your CU allocation. Instead it uses a 24-hour smoothing window:

  1. Your capacity has a CU budget reset every 24 hours
  2. A large Spark job that consumes 3× your hourly CUs will use up "future" CUs from later in the day
  3. If the debt isn't repaid (i.e., usage stays high all day), Fabric starts throttling — future operations are delayed, not cancelled
  4. Interactive queries (Power BI) get priority over background jobs (Spark, pipelines) during throttling

This means small-to-medium workloads with peaks can safely run on a smaller SKU without constant throttling — as long as the daily average stays within budget.

Cost Optimisation Strategies

1. Use Pay-as-you-go + Pause for dev/test

Dev and test workspaces can be assigned to a small F2 or F4 capacity. Pause it outside working hours (e.g., 7 PM to 7 AM). With 12 active hours per day, you pay about 50% of the always-on price.

2. Use Reserved Pricing for production

1-year reserved pricing saves roughly 40–45% over pay-as-you-go for production workloads that run 24/7. 3-year reserved saves up to 55%.

3. Separate dev and prod capacities

Running dev workloads (exploratory Spark jobs, pipeline testing) on the same capacity as production Power BI reports can cause throttling. Keep them on separate capacities with separate budgets.

4. Right-size with the Capacity Metrics app

Microsoft provides a free Microsoft Fabric Capacity Metrics app (install from AppSource). It shows CU consumption per workload, per item, and per time window — giving you the data to right-size confidently.

5. Use Fabric trials for POCs

Every Microsoft 365 tenant gets a 60-day Fabric trial equivalent to F64. Use this for proof-of-concept before committing to a paid SKU.

Key Takeaways

  • Fabric billing is based on Capacity Units (CUs) — a single meter for all workloads
  • F-SKUs range from F2 to F2048; F64 is the threshold for full Power BI Premium parity
  • Pay-as-you-go gives flexibility to pause; reserved pricing (1yr or 3yr) gives 40–55% savings
  • CU smoothing allows short bursts above the hourly limit without immediate throttling
  • Use the Capacity Metrics app to monitor consumption and right-size your SKU
  • Separate dev/test and production into different capacities to protect BI performance
